Learning Strategy

Optimization & Study Habits
sungjun@ubc~/learning➜ cat philosophy.txt
Learning, to me, is a process of optimization through reconstruction.

Inspired by scientific thinking and the Feynman Technique, I do not study by simply rereading material until it feels familiar.

Instead, I rebuild concepts in my own words, test whether I can explain them clearly, and use moments of confusion as signals of where my understanding is weak.

When I encounter a difficult idea, I isolate it, return to it deliberately, and refine it until it becomes something I can truly use.

In this way, I treat studying less as memorization and more as debugging thought—a cycle of building, testing, correcting, and improving.

My learning strategy is not about spending the most time, but about finding the most effective path to real understanding.

sungjun@ubc~/learning➜ cat scientist_methods.md
The study methods that influence me most are rooted in how scientists think:
  1. Optimization: finding the most effective path to understanding under limited time and energy.
  2. Feynman Technique: testing real understanding by explaining ideas in simple and clear language.
  3. Scientific Iteration: treating confusion and mistakes not as failure, but as feedback for improvement.

These approaches matter to me because they prioritize clarity, structure, and durable understanding over superficial familiarity.

sungjun@ubc~/learning➜ cat fold_and_resolve.md
Below is the study method that feels most natural to me, which I think of as Fold and Resolve:
  • First: I start by writing concepts down by hand so I can actively reconstruct what I am learning rather than just copy it.
  • Second: I explain the idea to myself in simple language, almost as if I were teaching someone else.
  • Third: If I get stuck on a proof, derivation, algorithm, or concept, I spend time trying to fully understand it on my own before moving on.
  • Fourth: If I still cannot resolve it, I mark it as unfinished and return to it later with more focus. The weak point becomes the target of my next review.
  • Fifth: After reviewing everything once, I revisit the difficult parts, resolve them, and then rebuild the topic again with a clearer structure.

For me, learning is not the passive accumulation of information, but an active cycle of testing, refining, and strengthening understanding until it becomes clear, connected, and usable.
